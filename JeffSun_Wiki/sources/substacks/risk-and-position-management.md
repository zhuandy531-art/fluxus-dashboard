## 1. Always Sell Some Into Strength

Throughout his years of streaming, Kristjan recommended selling pieces into strength — e.g., selling a piece every time the stock made another 5-10% move, then trailing the rest with the 10- or 20-day MA.

Jeff [explained](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1866483247930306837) the purpose of selling partials:
1. You lock in realised profit to reduce unrealised profit volatility.
2. You free up funds for new opportunities as the market continues to shape up.

As Oliver Kell puts it: "If you don't sell into strength, you'll sell into weakness."

### Jeff's Rules on Selling Into Strength

Jeff [shared](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1841852289742733701) his profit-taking system:
- He'll shave 33% off his position:
  - If the trade exceeds 2R in days 0-2; or
  - On day 3, if he's still in the trade (which automatically means the stock is above his average entry price).
- Where a trade is extended to the point that it reaches 10x ATR compared to the 50-day MA on the entry day, he'll take a further 33% off the table.
- If he's still in a trade beyond day 4, and the trade is 8-10x ATR extended to the 50-day MA, he takes another partial (33%).

### Psychological Benefits of Taking Partials

Jeff further hammers home the importance ([tweet](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1841852289742733701)):

> "As your equity grows (especially when it surpasses your absolute dollar risk tolerance relative to your monthly expenses), seeing unrealized profit drop from, say, +$500,000 to +$280,000 in a single session can be hard to handle, even if you're following textbook rules.
>
> "I believe not many can emotionally withstand holding 8-10 full-sized trades with 80% portfolio utilization, especially when trading with a bankroll that significantly impacts their quality of life and that of their dependents."

### Know What 'Normal' Looks Like

[Marios Stamatoudis](https://tradingresourcehub.substack.com/t/marios-stamatoudis) — [know the reality of your strategy](https://tradingresourcehub.substack.com/i/142212082/marios-stamatoudiss-perspective).

As Jeff [points out](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1867218691873804465), if you understand the 'normal' behaviour of the stocks you specialise in, you can adopt sell rules that align to that behaviour.

## 2. You Can't Outsmart the 10- and 20-Day MAs

Qullamaggie quotes:
- "The second you think you're smarter than the 10- and 20-day moving average, that's when you're doomed for mediocrity. Trust me. I talk from experience."
- "If you sell stuff too early in a hot market, then you retire 10 years later. You're never smarter than the 10- and 20-day moving average."
- "When you're in a trending market, and you get these leading stocks, the moving averages work like magic. They're literally magic lines."

### "You Do Not Sell Everything Until the Market Proves You Wrong."

Jeff [puts](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1866483247930306837) it:

> "You do not sell everything until the market proves you wrong.
>
> "Who'd have thought $KC could make a 200% gain in a month? Learn to hold your position and ride winners.
>
> "The outlier positions that can make an impact on your % performance are often those that you do not expect to make a huge move."

### Jeff's Rules on Trailing

From Jeff's [profit-taking system post](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1841852289742733701):
- On day 3, he moves his hard stop to breakeven.
- He'll also move to breakeven if the trade exceeds 4R prior to this day.
- From day 4 onwards, he uses the 10-day MA as a mental stop.
- If the stock closes below the 10-day MA (bearing in mind his Singapore timezone), he keeps his hard stop at breakeven, and lets the market trade for the first 5 minutes of the next day before moving his hard stop to the 5-minute opening range low (ORL).
- If the ORL never gets taken out that day, he'll move his stop back to breakeven before the market opens the next day, then set it back to that day's 5-minute ORL. He keeps repeating this process until his stop gets taken out.

> "The rationale is to hold onto your winning trade as long as possible with minimal sacrifice of unrealized profit."

## 3. If Your Stop Is Hit, Just Sell

Qullamaggie from Chat With Traders:

> "If I get stopped out, I get stopped out. I don't think, I just get out.
>
> "I've seen too many scenarios when things just keep going lower and you hesitate, and now suddenly your stop is twice as big as it would've been, and now it's three times as big and...
>
> "It just frees up mental power not to overthink things. I just get in, and I just get out."

### Jeff's Rules: Segregate Stop Losses

Jeff manages his downside on new positions by using three segregate stops:

*Source: [@jfsrevg](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1855927716404761065) on X*

He uses this system because getting stopped out of your full position at your original stop is rarely just -1R due to slippage, spread, and so on.

This allows him to reduce his losses to -0.6-0.8R — or, in the cases of gap downs, mitigate the risk of ending up with a multiple-R loss.

It aids with the *gradual* decline of your equity curve during a losing streak, and the *parabolic* growth during a strong winning streak.

*Source: [@jfsrevg](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1866778428709671171) on X*

But does his tight stop — particularly 'layer 1' — mean he gets shaken out of winners?

Jeff [explained](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1855927716404761065):

> "When I'm positioned in a trade that doesn't hit layer 1 (33%) or layer 2 (66%) stops within T+3, it's typically a strong runner, often yielding 5R+ based on my sell rules.
>
> "This year [2024], I've had winning trades as high as 51R, 4 trades at 20R+, and 11 at 10R+, with my max monthly win rate at a low of 31.6%.
>
> "I am wrong most of the time and there's a need to control drawdown, and downside risk in every new trade."

### Reducing vs Pausing Drawdown

Jeff [points out](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1855927716404761065):

> "Simply reducing trade frequency or stepping away from trading doesn't truly manage drawdown — it just pauses activity in your equity curve.
>
> "Avoiding action is an escape from reality, and to improve, it's essential to face issues directly and resolve them."

### Seeking Constant Improvement

How can you:
- Make more money on your winners?
- Lose less money on your losers?
- Generate more winning ideas?

As Stockbee says: "Good traders are innovators. They find better ways to do things."

## Limiting New Positions

[Another example](https://x.com/jfsrevg/status/1866483247930306837) from Jeff:

> "Another rule that I strongly urge everyone to explore and implement is reducing and setting a limit to your daily and/or weekly new position frequency.
>
> "Go to your journal and track that 1-3 days duration (MoM) that you are 'hyper' actively executing new position — is the outcome desirable? If not, taking on too many new positions will always be a trading pitfall, and you can fix this immediately.
>
> "I strongly believe in the edge of knowing when to go 'heavy' vs 'light' in terms of trade frequency and activity. I will be reluctant to new risk exposure when $SPY is 4x ATR% from 50-MA when it historically pulls back from 5x ATR% from 50-MA."

Christian Fromhertz:

> "If you've been doing this for a while, you actually like these little bit of pullbacks. You don't want to be buying things when the sentiment is super, super hot in momentum areas and in growth areas. You want things to come in a little bit, right? Because it resets things."

This relates to market cycles. The later you are within a swing cycle, the more prone to failure breakouts become — which is why Qullamaggie says things like 'we just need a quick flush'.
