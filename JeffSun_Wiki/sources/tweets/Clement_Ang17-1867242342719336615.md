1/ A Short Guide to Building Your Own Market Dashboard 

Thought I would finally do this post to help out the Fintwit community as this has been a request from quite a few of you for a while. The Market Dashboard was a tool I began using for myself sometime mid last year in 2023 as I was heavily inspired by @jfsrevg and his approach/commitment to the markets. 

At that point of time I was largely a boom and bust trader: having good trading periods, followed by getting trapped into a cycle of tilt as the market turns and I began to unnecessarily overtrade. The frustration mounted then as I heavily questioned whether I had what it takes to be a good trader - what was the point at being good at reading interpreting price/volume when I can't even keep the gains that I make? 

That said, rather than giving up - I began looking for a solution to get me out of this unregimented cycle. This was where the daily market plan plays into my process now: Detailing my interpretation of the market, as well as a quick post-session analysis of how I executed + approach going forward into the next trading session helped me establish even more structure around my approach to the markets. Thus far with over a year of doing these market plans, it has helped me loads in bringing consistency in my trading.

I thought to bring out the context of how this came about, because I believe that this is part of the journey that every aspiring professional trader will go through. There is no escaping this, and it really highlights why it is necessary for you to do the hard work yourself and go through this process. 

"Change happens when the pain of staying the same is greater than the pain of change" - Tony Robbins

Now lets get to the dashboard:

My approach to populating my Market Plans with data is to have a separate sheet with the data readily available. Here below, you can see that I actually have two files: The first is the excel sheet named 'US Market ETF Monitor', whilst the second is the actual 'Market Diary' itself.

Opening the ETF Monitor sheet (below), it looks like so, here I have essentially 4 segments: (1) Country ETFs, (2) S&P500 Sub-Market ETFs, (3) S&P500 Sector ETFs, both cap weighted and equal weighted, and lastly (4) Thematic/Group ETFs

To create this, I used a combination of Excel's in-built 'Stock History' function (you can search this up on google), as well as the 'Stocks' Data Type function:

The 'Stocks' Data Type function is pretty neat as it can help you obtain relevant data such as 52-Wk Low/High, Change %, Closing Price etc... automatically. That said, to be able to use it, you will need to compile yourself a ready list of ETFs to track on our own.

In addition to that, I use Stock History Data specifically to pull in historical stock prices from an ETF for a certain lookback period, such as the previous day close (P2D), the close 5 days ago (P5D), or the close at the beginning of the year (Year Start).

Here is an example of a formula I use (below), one tip I have here is to have an automated formula to spit of the date that you're looking to reference. That way, when using the 'Stock History' function, you have a way of directly referencing the cell, rather than having to manually key it into the function all the time - that helps you to 'automate' the dashboard.

The one I have opted for is via the workday function, as this will help to work around weekends (*note* this does not account for public holidays). For example, if I'm looking for the date 5 working days before, the formulate to use is as such: =WORKDAY(TODAY(),-5)
