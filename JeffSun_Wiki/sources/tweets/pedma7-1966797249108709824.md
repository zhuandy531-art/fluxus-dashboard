## Tweet Content

the other day @jfsrev asked me if I could add the caveat that for a breakout signal, what happens if we only take signals where the distance from entry to LoD (for longs) is not >60% of the ATR.

well, there's some interesting data I found that I'll share here.

but it's also worth noting that the way that Jeff trades is different than the test I'll be doing here. if I am not mistaken, Jeff takes positions as assets break out from consolidations, rather than waiting for EOD.

the test signal I am using here is a simple Close > breakout level at the end of day, which can be different fundamentally from Jeff's signal.

so for benchmark, we'll be using that standard approach of close>breakout.

I've also added a few features that were included in Jeff's approach such as:

> sell 1/2 of position 5 days after entry (if price>entry price)
> graduated stop losses, 33% each of the way until the final stop (so to not take the full "1R" loss)
> after time triggered sell, push stop loss to entry price.

this is the equity curve for such system in crypto.

here's some stats for the results.

total trades: 1,504
win rate on realized positions: 53%
avg win/loss: 1.24
sharpe: 1.12
cagr: 39%
max DD: 31%

so, now what if we add the rule that the total expansion from entry price away from its lod (for longs) and from hod (for shorts) can't be over 60%?

well, the performance declines quite a lot.

however, initially I kinda looked at it as Jeff's trades differently than waiting for the close, and in equities rather than crypto, so dynamics are probably the contributing factor for the difference.

but I wanted to try to justify this with some data.

what caught my attention is that indeed the signal is stronger on Jeff's version (ra_76) when you add the 60% atr filter.

meaning that it's more explanatory of future (vol normalized) returns than the signal without it (ra_75).

we can even look at this phenomenon by year. in general, Jeff's version does beat the simple version.

now why do we have such a decay in performance when it comes to the actual historical simulated returns of the strategy?

well, one of the reasons imo is that the number of trades decline a lot, from 1,504 trades on the initial signal, to 405 with this signal.

this means that we're not capturing some major trends, just by the law of large numbers.

then I also dug into the largest trades missed.

when the day ends, their vol expansion on that day, tends to be much higher than that 60% on the day.

this means that we end up missing a lot of large uptrends because of that one rule.

however like I mentioned above, Jeff does enter positions much earlier than this model, as the trade breaks out, rather than waiting for the full price expansion at close, much similarly to how I trade my own trend system.

and the significant improvement in signal quality could indicate that with that quasi-systematic approach to a breakout signal, it will boost the quality of the overall signals, without compromising the signals that are taken.
