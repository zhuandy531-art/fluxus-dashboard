## Tweet Content

I use my own excel trade journal primarily for recording trades, determining position sizes, and tracking my equity curve based on realized profit and loss. It is a very simple journal, but what sets it apart is that I include 33% and 66% price levels for stops from the entry point. I scale out of trades with unrealized losses at these 1/3 and 2/3 levels to avoid hitting the full stop. For me, a stop loss resulting in a 1R loss indicates a bad trade, while a good stop should minimize losses to around 0.3R to 0.5R. Usually after slippages, spread and cost, it's 0.6 to 0.7R. Cost is substantial in R factor and most people ignore it.

On a monthly basis, I compile and monitor the following metrics using formulas:

1. **Total Entry Trades:** This number might seem irrelevant at first, but for swing strategy if you have a significantly high turnover in certain months compared to your average data, those months are surely to be your losing ones. Successful trading months usually have low turnover, indicating a higher average holding period for winning trades and strong capital utilization.

2. **Stock vs. ETF Win Rate:** I separate my stock and ETF trade data to better understand my edge in each category. Honestly it may come to a point I will forgo and focus on just a single category in future if either of the category are proving to be the more profitable segment for me with a few more years of data. I want more luxury of time for hobbies and family.

3. **Net Absolute Profit/Loss and % After Costs:** This helps me illustrate why I prefer to long an inverse leveraged ETF (e.g., long $SQQQ) rather than short a conventional leveraged ETF (e.g., short $TQQQ). With liquidity already factored in, longing $SQQQ does not incur overnight borrowing costs.

4. **Max R Gain and Max R Loss:** I track these to study my biggest winners and to avoid repeating mistakes or biggest loser.

5. **Average Holding Period (Calendar Days) for Winning and Losing Trades:** This helps track my improvement in holding winning trades longer and finding ways to extend the holding period for winners.

6. **Gain to Pain Ratio for Stocks vs. ETFs.**

7. **Profit Factor (Dollar) for Stocks vs. ETFs.**

8. **Profit Factor (R Multiples) for Stocks vs. ETFs:** This value may differ from the dollar profit factor if you tend to adjust dollar risk during a drawdown in the middle of a month.

9. **Expectancy (Dollar):** This represents the average expected profit or loss per trade. This is a data you should always be improving on the upside and reduce the downside on bad months.

10. **Kelly Criterion:** Severe downswings require risk adjustment at the start of a new month to ensure survivability through potentially longer losing streaks.

There's another thing that I am exploring, which is implementing moving average line into my equity curve. But there's also drawback if you were to manually reduce $ risk just based on historic equity curve behavior. It does protect you to lose much slower but it has 0 prediction of the future. It may erode the absolute profit of your biggest year winning trade.
