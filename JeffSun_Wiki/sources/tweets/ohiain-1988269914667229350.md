## Tweet Content

There’s a fine line between catching the breakout and chasing the breakout, and most traders don’t know the difference until they’ve already paid for it.

Regarding chasing, one of the most valuable lessons I’ve learned came from @Qullamaggie, who said:

“When is it too late to get into the trade? 1x the average daily range. Try to keep your stops to one-third or half of the ADR.”

That single concept changed a lot for me.
If a stock has already moved 1x its ADR (Average Daily Range), odds are you’re late.

You’re no longer buying early momentum, but you’re buying someone else’s strength into resistance.

The breakout already happened.

That’s where most traders mess up.
They chase moves that are already extended, thinking they’re about to catch the next leg, when in reality, they’re just catching the tail end of the move.

For me, the fix was simple: I built strict guardrails to eliminate emotional entries.

Here's how I process my execution intraday:

Alongside ADR, I use a metric that @jfsrev talks about often: LOD Distance (Low of Day Distance).

I only want to enter when a stock is within <60% of its daily range from the low.

Anything beyond that and I’m chasing.

If price is near the top 40% of the day’s range and has already moved its full ADR, I know my stop will have to be too wide to make sense and that’s an immediate pass for me.

This combination (ADR + LOD Distance) helps me quantify my risk in real time.

It keeps me honest and forces me to ask:
“Am I buying tight structure… or am I just buying emotions?”

Here’s what this looks like in practice:
Let’s say a stock’s ADR is $5.

If it’s already up $5 on the day, it’s moved 1x ADR: that means it’s already pushed higher off my risk.

If my stop needs to be $2.50 away (half ADR), that’s acceptableish; however, I want it as tight as possible.

If I’m risking $4 to make $5, I’m chasing.
and that’s poor asymmetry.

The whole point of this process is to define where in the move I want to participate.

I want to enter as soon as the stock starts to break out, not after. This has completely changed the way I trade.

Before, I used to get sucked into strength, chasing green candles because it “looked strong.”
Now, I have data and parameters that tell me when I’m too late.

It’s not emotional anymore.

On very rare occasions, I’ll break the rule, but only when the setup is exceptional (maybe 1% of my trades): major catalyst, institutional volume, perfect structure.

Otherwise, I sit on my hands.
What this has given me is clarity.

It’s not about catching every move for me, but it’s about catching the ones that fit my system.

And for me, these metrics have become non-negotiable filters that keep me disciplined, focused, and risk-aware.

If you find yourself constantly chasing, build objective data points into your system.

1x ADR%. @Qullamaggie 
<60% LOD%. @jfsrev 
Stop width.
Whatever else fits your style.

The most important thing isn’t how much you make, it’s how tight you can keep your risk while giving yourself room to win.

You’ll stop forcing trades.
You’ll stop chasing.

And you’ll finally start seeing consistent progress.
