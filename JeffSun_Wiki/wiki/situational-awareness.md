# Situational Awareness

> [!summary]
> Jeff treats situational awareness as an objective, quantifiable discipline -- not gut feel. He uses ATR% extension from the 50-MA on SPY, breadth indicators, and top-down sector analysis to decide when to go heavy vs. light on new positions. Pairing situational awareness with execution is what separates consistent performers from those who only have good setups.

## Core Concept

Situational awareness is the practice of reading the broader market environment before committing capital to individual trades. Jeff frames this as Chapter 5 of his trading process -- the bridge between having a focus list and actually executing. The key insight: situational awareness can be **objective**, not subjective. It is built from measurable data points including breadth, ATR% extension on indexes, relative strength across sectors, and new highs vs. new lows.

Jeff states plainly: "To significantly boost your annualized performance, you also need to pair situational awareness" with your execution and trade management skills.

## Rules / Details

**Limiting new positions based on SPY extension:**

Jeff is reluctant to take on new risk exposure when SPY is 4x ATR% from the 50-MA, because historically the index pulls back from the 5x ATR% level. This is a concrete, repeatable rule -- not a feeling about the market being "too high."

From Jeff's own words:

> "I strongly believe in the edge of knowing when to go 'heavy' vs 'light' in terms of trade frequency and activity. I will be reluctant to new risk exposure when $SPY is 4x ATR% from 50-MA when it historically pulls back from 5x ATR% from 50-MA."

**Diversifying correlation risk:**

When the market environment is uncertain, Jeff diversifies correlation risk with lower-beta ideas rather than concentrating entirely in high-beta momentum names. This reduces the portfolio's sensitivity to a single market-wide reversal.

**Annotating trades on the index chart:**

Jeff recommends annotating every trade -- wins and losses -- directly on the index chart. This exercise reveals how your trading activity correlates with market conditions. Over time, patterns emerge: you may discover that your losing streaks cluster during extended markets, or your best trades come after pullbacks.

**High volatility is not a reason to sit in cash:**

Jeff emphasizes: "High volatile environment shouldn't be a day you stay in cash -- you need to know when to hunt." His biggest shorts of 2025 came on March 28th, during peak volatility. The point is not to avoid volatile markets but to adapt your approach to them -- going short when conditions warrant it, going light on longs when extension is high, and staying active in screening.

## How Jeff Applies It

Jeff's daily process integrates situational awareness at multiple levels:

1. **Pre-market**: He checks SPY's ATR% multiple from the 50-MA to gauge extension or oversold conditions
2. **Breadth check**: He reviews Qullamaggie's breadth indicator, TradingView breadth symbols, and MMTW (% of stocks above 20-MA) for a bird's-eye view
3. **Top-down scan**: He works through equal-weight vs. cap-weight indexes, then sectors, then industry groups
4. **Position frequency limit**: He caps himself at no more than 3 new positions per session (Chapter 6, Hard Rule #9) and rolls risk as the market confirms prior-day momentum
5. **Activity during volatility**: When market volatility picks up, he stays highly active in managing his watchlist and focus list -- updating it daily, sometimes multiple times per day

The result is that Jeff's trade frequency and sizing are a direct function of market conditions, not emotion. When conditions are favorable, he goes heavy. When the market is extended, he goes light or shifts to short exposure.
