# Progressive Exposure

> [!summary]
> Rooted in Mark Minervini's concept, progressive exposure means trading your biggest size when your trading is at its best, and your smallest when conditions are unfavorable. Jeff Sun draws a critical distinction: reducing exposure during drawdowns is valid risk management, but simply stepping away from the market is not — it is avoidance, and it prevents the improvement that comes from active engagement.

## Core Concept

Progressive exposure is the portfolio-level counterpart to position-level risk management. While the [[3-stop-strategy]] and [[t-plus-3-framework]] manage individual trade risk, progressive exposure governs how much total capital is deployed at any given time.

The principle is straightforward: your equity curve compounds fastest when you take your largest positions during your highest-conviction, highest-performance periods. Conversely, during drawdowns or unfavorable market conditions, reducing position size limits the damage.

But Jeff adds a nuance that separates his approach from simple "sit on your hands" advice:

> "Simply reducing trade frequency or stepping away from trading doesn't truly manage drawdown — it just pauses activity in your equity curve. Avoiding action is an escape from reality, and to improve, it's essential to face issues directly and resolve them."
> — [[sources/tweets/jfsrev-1855927716404761065|Tweet]]

## Rules / Details

### When to Trade Heavy

Jeff goes aggressive when multiple conditions align:

1. **Market breadth is favorable** — the majority of stocks are above key moving averages, new highs outpace new lows
2. **Personal win rate is trending up** — journal data confirms the current approach is working
3. **High-quality setups are abundant** — screeners are producing multiple A-rated ideas with proper VCP, RVOL confirmation, and tight entries
4. **Existing portfolio is durable** — positions beyond T+3 are trending with breakeven stops, providing a profit cushion

### When to Trade Light

1. **Market is extended** — SPY at 4-5x ATR% from 50-MA historically precedes pullbacks
2. **Win rate is declining** — journal review shows increasing stop-outs
3. **Setups are scarce or low-quality** — screeners returning few names, or breakouts are failing
4. **Personal drawdown exceeds tolerance** — but even here, Jeff does not stop trading entirely

### The Distinction: Reducing vs. Pausing

This is Jeff's most important contribution to the concept. Many traders are told to "step away" during drawdowns. Jeff argues this is counterproductive:

- **Reducing drawdown** = trading smaller size, being more selective, tightening entry criteria. You remain engaged with the market and continue building data in your journal.
- **Pausing activity** = stepping away entirely. Your equity curve flatlines, but you learn nothing. When you return, you face the same problems plus rust from inactivity.

> "Avoiding action is an escape from reality, and to improve, it's essential to face issues directly and resolve them."

### Limiting New Position Frequency

Jeff implements progressive exposure partly through hard caps on new positions:

> "Another rule that I strongly urge everyone to explore and implement is reducing and setting a limit to your daily and/or weekly new position frequency. Go to your journal and track that 1-3 days duration (MoM) that you are 'hyper' actively executing new position — is the outcome desirable?"
> — [[sources/tweets/jfsrev-1866483247930306837|Tweet]]

His hard rule: no more than 3 new positions per session. He rolls risk as the market confirms prior entries' momentum.

## How Jeff Applies It

### Using ATR% for Market-Level Signals

Jeff uses his ATR% multiple from 50-MA indicator on the index level as an objective measure of market extension:

> "I will be reluctant to new risk exposure when $SPY is 4x ATR% from 50-MA when it historically pulls back from 5x ATR% from 50-MA."
> — [[sources/tweets/jfsrev-1866483247930306837|Tweet]]

This is not a binary on/off switch — it modulates his aggressiveness. At 2-3x ATR%, he is fully aggressive. At 4x, he is cautious with new entries. At 5x+, he may actively look for short opportunities (as he did in March 2025).

### The Constant Improvement Mandate

Even during reduced exposure periods, Jeff insists on working to improve:

- Make more money on your winners?
- Lose less money on your losers?
- Generate more winning ideas?

As Stockbee says: **"Good traders are innovators. They find better ways to do things."**

The fisherman metaphor captures this philosophy:

> "When fishermen cannot go to the sea, they repair nets."
> — [[sources/tweets/jfsrev-1894192503219523789|Tweet]]
