# Market Cycles

> [!summary]
> Jeff and the traders he references understand that markets move in swing cycles, and that a trader's behavior must adapt to where the cycle currently sits. Later in a swing cycle, breakouts become more prone to failure. Pullbacks are not threats -- they reset sentiment and create the next generation of setups. During boring or unfavorable markets, the disciplined response is to prepare, not force trades.

## Core Concept

Markets do not trend indefinitely. They move in cycles of expansion, exhaustion, pullback, and reset. The practical implication for breakout traders is stark: the same setup that works beautifully early in a cycle will fail late in a cycle. Recognizing where you are in the cycle is not optional -- it directly determines position sizing, trade frequency, and whether to be aggressive or defensive.

This is why Qullamaggie says things like "we just need a quick flush." He is not hoping for a crash. He is recognizing that a pullback resets the conditions needed for breakouts to work again. Without pullbacks, the market becomes extended, breakouts fail into overhead supply, and momentum traders get chopped up.

## Rules / Details

**Later-cycle breakout failure:**

The later you are within a swing cycle, the more prone to failure breakouts become. This is because:

- Stocks are already extended from their moving averages
- Buyers who entered early are sitting on large gains and looking to sell
- New buyers are chasing at elevated levels with tight stops that get triggered on any weakness
- The pool of stocks setting up fresh VCP patterns shrinks as the cycle ages

Jeff addresses this through his hard rules on position frequency and ATR% extension limits. When SPY is 4x ATR% from the 50-MA, he is already reducing new long exposure because the cycle is getting long in the tooth.

**Pullbacks reset sentiment:**

Christian Fromhertz captures this clearly:

> "If you've been doing this for a while, you actually like these little bit of pullbacks. You don't want to be buying things when the sentiment is super, super hot in momentum areas and in growth areas. You want things to come in a little bit, right? Because it resets things."

Pullbacks serve a structural purpose: they shake out weak holders, create new bases, reset moving averages, and bring the ATR% extension back to neutral. The next wave of breakouts emerges from the bases that form during the pullback. Without the pullback, there are no new bases and no new breakouts.

**"We just need a quick flush" -- Qullamaggie:**

This phrase encapsulates the breakout trader's relationship with drawdowns. A quick, sharp flush is preferable to a slow grind because it resets conditions faster. The worst environment for breakout traders is not a crash -- it is a slow, grinding, choppy market that never fully pulls back and never fully trends.

**"When fishermen cannot go to sea, they repair nets":**

Jeff uses this metaphor for what to do during boring or unfavorable markets. When the market is not producing setups, the disciplined response is not to force trades. Instead, it is to:

- Review your journal and trading data
- Refine your screeners and watchlist process
- Study historical setups and past trades
- Work on the spreadsheets, tools, and systems that support your process
- Maintain your routine so you are ready when conditions improve

This is one of Jeff's core beliefs from Chapter 10 of his Complete Guide. The temptation during boring markets is to lower your standards and take mediocre setups. The correct move is to do nothing in the market and everything outside of it.

**"You don't need to play every hand":**

Jeff explicitly states that you do not need to play every hand. Sitting out when conditions are unfavorable is itself a position. Your equity curve benefits from the trades you do not take during bad conditions just as much as it benefits from the trades you do take during good conditions.

## How Jeff Applies It

Jeff's cycle awareness manifests in concrete actions:

1. **Early cycle (post-flush)**: He is aggressive with new positions, sizing up, and building a portfolio. Breakouts have the highest probability of follow-through because bases are fresh and the market has reset
2. **Mid cycle**: He maintains positions that are working, is selective with new entries, and begins prioritizing adding to existing winners over initiating new positions
3. **Late cycle (extended)**: He limits new long exposure (max 3 per session, reluctant at 4x ATR% SPY extension), takes more partials into strength, and shifts focus to short ideas via inverse ETFs
4. **Boring/choppy markets**: He reduces activity, repairs nets -- reviewing journals, refining tools, studying setups -- and waits patiently for the next cycle to begin
5. **During pullbacks**: He actively manages his watchlist and focus list, screening for the new bases that are forming. The pullback is when the next batch of leaders reveals itself
